Where the Rainbow Ends

Feb 6, 2026 By Lin

The Infinite Playground

Looking back, my childhood in the province wasn't just good; it was a constant, delirious high of happiness. I remember running through the fields behind my Lola’s house, my chest bursting with laughter that felt like it would never end. The world was a playground built specifically for me. If I scraped my knee, Lola was there with a warm hand and a sweet treat to make the pain vanish. We didn't have much, but I didn't know that. I thought we were rich because I had the sun, the mud, and the endless energy of being young. I truly believed that life was just one long game of patintero, where even if you got tagged, you could always just start over.

Jagged Edges and Deadlines

That era of my life feels like a vivid, technicolor dream now. I remember the afternoons spent waiting for the sound of the sorbetero bell, believing that a scoop of cheese ice cream on a stale bun was the peak of culinary luxury. I remember catching spiders in matchboxes and feeling like a beast tamer, or lying on the bamboo floor while the electric fan hummed a lullaby, convinced that the only job I would ever have was to be happy. I never saw the bills piling up on the table. I never noticed the way the adults whispered about rice prices or harvest yields. I was insulated in a bubble of pure joy, protected by my elders who silently absorbed the blows of the world so I wouldn't have to feel them.

But growing up is a slow, painful process of waking up. Now that I am in college, the bubble has not just popped; it has been shredded. The silence of the province has been replaced by the frantic noise of deadlines and the hum of my laptop fan at 3 AM. I sit here, staring at lines of code or endless pages of readings, and the clarity is suffocating. I finally understand the value of money—not as a concept, but as a terrifying reality. I catch myself calculating the cost of my meals against my allowance, realizing that the "simple life" I enjoyed was paid for by the sweat and sacrifice of people I never properly thanked.

The world isn't soft anymore. It's a jagged place of expectations and performance. There are no "cupcakes and rainbows" here; there is only the pressure to succeed, the fear of failure, and the realization that mediocrity is a death sentence in this economy. I used to think stress was just being called on in class; now I know it as a physical weight that sits on my chest when I try to sleep. The colorful imagination that once turned shadows into monsters has been repurposed to imagine worst-case scenarios for my future. I am learning, painfully, that comfort is expensive, and peace of mind is a luxury I can no longer afford.

Looking Back Over the Fence

I often close my eyes and try to go back to that backyard, to the smell of Lola’s cooking and the feeling of grass under my feet. I want to tell that little kid to run slower, to laugh louder, to hold onto that moment with a death grip.

Because once you learn that the world isn't fair, once you see the strings holding up the scenery, you can never unsee them. I am building a future, yes, but I would give anything to go back to the days when I didn't know how much that future cost.
